How to Choose a Racket

Choosing the right racket is the single most important equipment decision a tennis player can make. The wrong racket can limit your development, cause arm injuries, and reduce your enjoyment of the game. The right one accelerates progress and feels like a natural extension of your arm.

The key is to match the racket to your current skill level, not the level you aspire to reach. Many beginners mistakenly buy professional-level rackets that require precise technique to use effectively.

Beginner

Light frame · Head-heavy balance · 105+ sq in head size · Generates power with less technique

Intermediate

Medium weight · Even balance · 100–105 sq in · Balance of power and control

Professional

Heavy frame · Head-light balance · 95–100 sq in · Maximum control and feel

Tip: When demoing rackets, play at least 15–20 balls with each one before forming an opinion. First impressions can be misleading.

Grip Size Guide

An incorrectly sized grip is one of the most common causes of tennis elbow and arm fatigue. To find your grip size, hold a ruler along your dominant hand with the end at the bottom horizontal crease of your palm. Measure to the tip of your ring finger.

Quick test

Hold the racket with your normal forehand grip. With your other hand, try to insert your index finger into the gap between your palm and fingers. If it fits snugly — it's correct. Too tight means too small; too loose means too large.

Tip: When in doubt, choose the smaller size — you can always add an overgrip to increase thickness by approximately 1/16".

Weight & Balance

Racket weight affects both power and comfort. Heavier rackets (300g+) deliver more power and stability but require more strength and technique. Lighter rackets (under 275g) are easier to manoeuvre but can transmit more shock to the arm.

⬆️
Head-heavy

More power · Better for beginners · More arm stress

⚖️
Even balance

Versatile · Good for intermediate · All-court play

⬇️
Head-light

More control · Less arm fatigue · For advanced players

String Patterns

The string pattern refers to the number of main strings (vertical) × cross strings (horizontal). A 16×19 pattern creates more space between strings, generating more spin potential. An 18×20 pattern is denser, providing greater control and durability but less spin.

16×19 Open pattern
  • More spin potential
  • Livelier feel
  • Strings break faster
  • Best for recreational players
18×20 Dense pattern
  • Maximum control
  • Consistent string bed
  • Strings last longer
  • Preferred by advanced players

Tips for Players

🎾 Demo before you buy

Always try before committing. Many pro shops offer demo programmes.

🪢 Restring every 3 months

Strings lose tension over time. Restring as many times per year as you play per week.

🌡️ Protect from heat

Avoid leaving rackets in hot cars. Extreme heat warps frames and ruins string tension.

📐 Mid-tension to start

String at the mid-point of the recommended tension range when starting out.

Pro tip: If your arm feels sore after playing, consider stringing at a lower tension. Lower tension = more dwell time = less shock on impact.
